Unequal Ground

Power, land, and life in early America. The ideals said all men are born free and equal. The data tells a different story.

This exhibit uses primary sources from HIST 160 alongside historical data to map the gap between the promise of freedom in early American political thought and the reality lived by Indigenous peoples and enslaved Africans. Each chart caption notes which sources are from the course and which are outside.

01  ·  The Promise

All Men Are Born Free and Equal

John Locke's Second Treatise of Government (1689) built the philosophical scaffolding that the American founders borrowed almost directly. In his framework, people begin in a "state of nature," all equal and independent, bound only by natural law. Government is legitimate only with the consent of the governed. No one can rightfully rule another without their agreement.

These weren't abstract ideas. Massachusetts put them into its 1780 Constitution. Jefferson distilled them into the Declaration. They were the official ideology of the new republic.

"Men being, as has been said, by nature, all free, equal, and independent, no one can be put out of this estate and subjected to the political power of another without his own consent."
John Locke, Second Treatise of Government (1689), Ch. 8, §95

But Locke's "all men" was a narrow category in practice. His labor theory of property, the idea that land becomes yours when you mix your labor with it in the European agricultural sense, was used to deny Indigenous peoples title to their own territories. The logic: intensive European-style farming was "real" labor; Indigenous land use was not. Therefore the land was unowned. Therefore it could be taken.

The Promise

Locke: "all men are by nature free, equal, and independent." The Massachusetts Constitution (1780): "all men are born free and equal." The Declaration (1776): "all men are created equal."

The Reality

In 1790, 18% of people in the United States were legally owned as property. The majority of the rest were excluded from political participation by sex, race, or lack of property. The "all" in "all men" excluded most people.

02  ·  Epidemic

The Great Rash

Book XII of the Florentine Codex, compiled between 1540 and 1570 from Nahuatl testimony and translated by James Lockhart, describes the smallpox epidemic that swept through Tenochtitlan in 1520. The Nahuatl term was hueyzahuatl, the great rash. It arrived before Cortés launched his final military campaign. It killed tens of thousands, including the newly chosen tlatoani Cuitláhuac, who died within eighty days of taking office.

"There came among us a great sickness, a general plague... The pustules that covered people caused great desolation; a great many people died of it. They could no longer walk about, but lay in their dwellings and sleeping places, no longer able to move or stir."
Book XII of the Florentine Codex (compiled c. 1540–1570), trans. James Lockhart — adapted from course reading

What the Codex makes clear is that the epidemic wasn't a side note to Spanish military conquest. It was intertwined with it. Disease arrived via Spanish contact and spread through a population with no prior immunity. It preceded the final siege. It shaped who survived to resist and who didn't.

This pattern repeated across the continent for two centuries. Epidemic contact preceded, accompanied, or followed European expansion everywhere it went. By the time many regions were formally "settled" by Europeans, the populations that had lived there for generations had already been decimated.

03  ·  Enslaved

The Numbers Behind the System

By 1860 there were nearly four million enslaved people in the United States. That number didn't emerge from nowhere. It grew over two centuries, accelerating sharply in the 1800s even after the international slave trade was banned in 1808. Slavery was a system that reproduced itself.

The legal infrastructure that sustained this growth is documented in the course reading. The South Carolina Slave Code of 1740, passed one year after the Stono Rebellion (1739), systematized what had been accumulating over decades of colonial law. It made enslaved people chattel, personal property "to all intents, constructions, and purposes." It prohibited assembly, prohibited literacy, and gave enslavers near-total legal authority over the people they claimed to own.

"All negroes and Indians... mulattoes or mustizoes who are now or shall hereafter be in this Province, and all their issue and offspring... shall be and they are hereby declared to be, and remain forever hereafter, absolute slaves, and shall follow the condition of the mother."
South Carolina Slave Code of 1740 — course primary source

The Stono Rebellion context matters. Enslaved people didn't passively accept enslavement. The Code was a legislative response to organized resistance. In 1739, enslaved people in South Carolina rose up, killed white colonists, and marched south toward Spanish Florida, where freedom had been promised to escaped slaves. The legislature doubled down precisely because the system was being challenged.

04  ·  The Land

Census as Instrument of Extraction

The Nahuatl Census from the Cuernavaca region, compiled around 1540 and translated by Lisa Sousa, is a colonial document in the most direct sense. It records calpulli (community) units household by household: who lived there, how much land they farmed (in irrigated versus hill plots, measured in brazas), whether residents were baptized, and what they owed.

The obligations flow to Martín Molotecatl teuctli, a local lord operating within the Spanish colonial system. The census doesn't exist to understand people. It exists to enumerate what can be extracted from them: labor, agricultural output, tribute. The act of counting is the act of claiming.

The census records each household's irrigated and hill maize fields, their composition, their baptismal status, and the tribute obligations owed to the local lord. Every brazas of land, every unbaptized resident is a data point in a system designed to measure what can be taken.
Nahuatl Census, Cuernavaca Region (c. 1540), trans. Lisa Sousa — paraphrase from course reading

The same logic operated across the continent under different names. Land that Indigenous peoples had governed, farmed, hunted, and ceremonially maintained for generations was reclassified as empty or unowned under Locke's labor theory and European legal frameworks. 05  ·  Resistance

Who Pushed Back

The systems documented in this exhibit were contested. Not everyone accepted them. The record of early America includes people who resisted, challenged, and refused.

Elizabeth Freeman, 1781 Course Reading

Enslaved in Massachusetts, she heard the new state constitution read aloud: "all men are born free and equal." She hired a lawyer and sued for her freedom. The court found in her favor. She was among the first enslaved people to win freedom through legal argument under the republic's own founding documents.

Stono Rebellion, 1739 Course Context

Enslaved people in South Carolina organized, rose up, and marched south toward Spanish Florida and freedom. The colonial legislature's direct response was the Slave Code of 1740. Resistance and repression shaped each other continuously.

"What, to the American slave, is your 4th of July? I answer: a day that reveals to him, more than all other days in the year, the gross injustice and cruelty to which he is the constant victim. To him, your celebration is a sham; your boasted liberty, an unholy license; your national greatness, swelling vanity."
Frederick Douglass, "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?" Rochester, New York, July 5, 1852 — course reading

Douglass delivered this in 1852, 76 years after the Declaration. He didn't argue the ideals were wrong. He argued the republic had failed to live up to them, that the contradiction between the promise and the reality was visible to anyone willing to look.

That's what this exhibit tries to do. Look at the contradiction directly. The ideals of early American political thought were real. So was the world built alongside them, on stolen land, with stolen labor, sustained by law and violence. Both things are true. That's the point.
